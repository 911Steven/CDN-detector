Can a stupid teenage mistake really be considered statutory rape?

Owen Labrie, a star graduate of an elite New Hampshire preparatory school, was sentenced to a year in jail and lifetime registration as a sex offender last week. His crime? Having sexual contact with a 15-year-old when he was 18.

That phrase, “sexual contact,” is key. Labrie and the victim have varying accounts of what happened. He says it was little more than making out and dry humping; she says there was full penetration. Regardless, the scene reads in no way like a clear-cut case of rape, as the Washington Post’s Ruth Marcus explains:

She testified that she told Labrie to stop — but also that she lifted her hips so he could remove her shorts. “He couldn’t know that I was uncomfortable because I was laughing,” she told police. She told a school nurse the intercourse was consensual and exchanged friendly e-mails with Labrie after the encounter (“You’re quite the angel yourself”). He claimed at trial there was no penetration, although he allegedly bragged to friends afterward that he had “boned” the freshman.

Labrie was acquitted of felony rape because of those ambiguous details, but still convicted of a misdemeanor for statutory rape and a felony for using a computer to lure a minor for sex.

For this, Labrie, the former captain of the school soccer team, has had his life effectively ruined. Once a student admitted to Harvard on full scholarship, Labrie will now find himself in a Concord jail instead of a Cambridge dormitory next year. His prospects after prison seem just as bleak. As a registered sexual offender for life, he’ll doubtlessly have trouble applying for jobs.

Is this really fair?

Don’t get me wrong, Labrie is no angel. In fact, the very reason he hooked up with a 15-year-old was to participate in a creepy school tradition in which upperclassmen have sex with as many underclassmen as they can, dubbed the “senior salute.” But should a bright young man be punished for the rest of his life for such juvenile behavior — especially when his victim agreed to hook up with him, failed to express her discomfort, and sent flirty messages the next day?

The sentencing seems downright unjust when you get down to the nitty gritty of each count, as Marcus does. On his statutory rape conviction:

In an era when more than a quarter of girls report having had sex by the time of their 16th birthday, the pool of potential criminals is enormous. Should the difference of a few months — that she was not yet 16, that he had turned 18 — be the sole determinant of whether Labrie’s behavior was criminal?

On his felony for luring a minor for sex with a computer:

More troubling is the felony count on which Labrie was convicted, contributing to his sentence of five years’ probation. This law was intended to prevent sexual predators from trolling the Internet — not to punish teenagers for communicating online.

For these two reasons, Marcus characterizes Labrie’s conviction as “cruel and unusual punishment,” and I’m inclined to agree. Certainly the laws that convicted Labrie were passed with the good intention of shielding minors from predators. But Labrie is no predator and was a minor himself just months before the so-called assault. The road to hell is clearly paved with good intentions.

States need to get smarter about statutory rape. A handful have passed what are called “Romeo and Juliet laws,” which prevent an adult from being convicted of statutory rape if he or she is within a certain age range of a minor. If New Hampshire had such a law in place, 18-year-old Labrie could not have been convicted of consensual sex with a 15-year-old.

Unfortunately, these reasonable exceptions are likely going to become harder to pass, given that many activists are calling on states to raise the bar for consent (see: California). Something must change soon before more young men’s lives are ruined because of one stupid teenage mistake.
