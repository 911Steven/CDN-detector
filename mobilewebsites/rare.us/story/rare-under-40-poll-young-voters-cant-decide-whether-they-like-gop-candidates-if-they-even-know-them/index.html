Rare Under 40 poll: Young voters can’t decide whether they like GOP candidates — if they even know them

Louisiana Republican Gov. Bobby Jindal announced Wednesday that he was entering the 2016 presidential race while trying to distinguish himself in a growing field of more than a dozen candidates packed with better known rivals.

As Jindal and other party leaders announce their candidacy ahead of the 2016 presidential election campaign season, an exclusive Rare Under 40 poll takes the pulse of younger Americans to see who they like or don’t like — or if they know.

Republicans officially running for president in 2016, so far, are: 

  • Jeb Bush, former Florida governor
  • Ben Carson, retired neurosurgeon
  • U.S. Sen. Ted Cruz, R-Texas
  • Carly Fiorina, former business exec for Hewlett-Packard
  • U.S. Sen. Lindsey Graham, R-South Carolina
  • Mike Huckabee, former Arkansas governor
  • Bobby Jindal, Louisiana governor
  • George Pataki, former New York governor
  • U.S. Sen. Rand Paul, R-Kentucky
  • Rick Perry, former Texas governor
  • U.S. Sen. Marco Rubio, R-Florida
  • Rick Santorum, former U.S. senator from Pennsylvania
  • Donald Trump, real estate mogul

The Rare Under 40 poll found that voters of all ages are divided on whether they like Republican leaders, that’s if they even know them at all … mostly likely because of the broad field of candidates. Young people had more agreement on likable leaders in the narrower Democratic field of candidates. But overall, half of voters don’t like front-runner Hillary Clinton either.

There was no party leader among Republicans or Democrats with an overall majority of likes in the Rare Under 40 poll.

Among young voters, 32 percent say they like Marco Rubio — the most of any GOP leader the poll questioned. However, even more (43 percent) still said they didn’t like him — and 25 percent said they didn’t know.

Rubio did get a rare majority of likes among young Republicans (55 percent), compared with 16 percent of Democrats and 26 percent of independent voters. However, among young black voters, he also had a majority of dislikes: 69 percent.

Mike Huckabee and Rand Paul scored near the top with 29 percent each.

Huckabee was most likable for a majority of young Republicans (53 percent) and evangelical Christians (61 percent), while Paul only came close to a majority with 43 percent of young Republicans.

Pulling likability over 50 percent in a crowded candidate field is tough, and the Rare Under 40 poll confirmed that. But a majority of young evangelical Christians also liked Ben Carson (58 percent) and Ted Cruz (55 percent).

So which GOP leaders don’t young voters like? Most agreed they don’t like Sarah Palin (58 percent), Jeb Bush (53 percent) or Chris Christie (52 percent) — and voters over 40 agreed.

The majority of voters of all ages also agreed on one other thing: They either don’t know or have no opinion of John Kasich, Republican governor of Ohio, or business executive Carly Fiorina.

About the poll

From May 23 to May 26, Gravis Marketing, a nonpartisan research firm, conducted a random survey of 3,682 voters in the United States regarding current issues that impact the political and social landscape. The poll was conducted using Gravis Marketing Internet Panels and Gravis Automated Calls to landlines and cellphones on behalf of online media outlet Rare.us. Overall, the poll has a margin of error of ±2 percentage points and was weighted by select demographic characteristics. The general scope of this Gravis Marketing poll was to capture the likability of presidential candidates and party leaders among registered voters under the age of 40. The poll included a group of 52% female registered voters and 48% male voters of all ages across the United States. Before the data were weighted to fairly represent results, 38% of those polled stated they were members of the Democratic Party, while 32% identified as Republicans and 29% as ‘independent’ voters.

Rare’s previous in-depth scientific survey asked a range of cultural questions, looking for insights on race, religion, politics, same-sex marriage, climate change and the direction of our country.

Why yellow? Rare.us is "going yellow" Nov. 5-11 in support of veterans causes. You may be familiar with the yellow "Support our Troops" ribbon. To Rare, yellow reminds us of the courage and valor of our veterans, and by transforming our look this week from red to yellow, we hope to draw more attention to the causes close to veterans.

We are partnering with Team Red, White and Blue to tell the amazing stories of these men and women who are thriving and making a difference in their communities every day, and to remind America that veterans are not victims. Team Red, White and Blue's mission is to enrich the lives of America's veterans by connecting them to their communities through physical and social activity.
